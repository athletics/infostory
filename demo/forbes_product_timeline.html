May 2010

Forbes acquires True/Slant

Funded in June 2008, True/Slant set out to disrupt the traditional media model for news and information. The goal: build a digital platform for journalists and topic experts to self-publish and attract audiences to their individual brands. Each was part of an incentive-based compensation program tied to building a loyal following. As we evolved, I had to answer a question. Was T/S destined to be a stand-alone website or a catalyst for change within a traditional media brand? Our team realized FORBES could provide a larger stage for our ideas. Besides, as a product guy, I didn't relish the idea of raising more money and running a sales team.

August 2010

Individually-branded article pages released

With a sense of urgency, we re-branded the T/S article page for FORBES. Staff reporters and contributors began using our highly customized WordPress tools to write, publish and market their content across the social Web. The launch was marred by frustrating publishing outages. T/S code that scaled to 1.5 million users wasn't quite ready for the much larger FORBES audience nor the different server configurations and hardware in place at that time. A dedicated Tech team didn't stop working until all was resolved.

September 2010

Launched profile platform for 6,000 people, places and companies

I believe strongly in starting from the core and building out. That means iteration and continuous product releases, with all the compromises that entails. As I like to say, never try to boil the ocean. The FORBES 400, our franchise list, provided the perfect opportunity to strike fast by building a scalable people profile page. That screen -- with real-time news, data and community -- became the foundation for our popular lists about people, places, companies and organizations. It also set the stage for a new technology platform and content management system.

November 2010

AdVoice launched with SAP

FORBES believes that content is content, and that the key to digital publishing in the era of social media is transparency and authenticity. Our AdVoice partners publish using the same tools as staffers and contributors. Their content is dynamically integrated and easily discoverable across our platform, always clearly labeled as marketer content. At first, many staffers were taken aback by the likes of SAP publishing content alongside them. Now, most see AdVoice as an innovative ad product that is right for the times.

June 2011

The “New Newsroom” begins

The New Newsroom was critical to changing the DNA at FORBES. We made organizational changes based on the need for a feedback loop structured around data analysis, audience development, SEO, SMO and a producer desk to support 1,000 content creators. In another unusual move for a traditional media company, we relocated a member of the Sales team to the newsroom in an effort to better fulfill ad campaigns.

July 2011

Real-time stats dashboard v1.0

I like to say the tools will set you free, the data will help your soar. With more contributors and a larger audience we needed to rebuild our statistics engine to give content creators and editors better visibility into individual performance and audience behavior. Frankly, it also kept breaking. That was bad, and this was bitter sweet: journalists who once chafed at our public page view counter actually stopped publishing when it crashed. The feedback loop had become that important to them.

August 2011

Individually branded article page v2.0

When we started True/Slant, I wanted to build the best individually branded article page for contributors on the Web. This iteration got us closer to that goal. It's easy to junk up a Web screen with links, modules and so much more. The hard part is throwing elements out. Our new page puts the most important elements front and center and provides elegant contributor branding. The launch of this screen kicked off a seven-month run to 30 million monthly unique users, double the 15 million users in February 2010.

October 2011

Introduced contributor tools for promoting related content

Any algorithm can be improved with a layer of human decision making. So says Steve McNally, a member of our Tech team who focuses on publishing tools. That's why we're so committed to giving journalists more creative power to program their pages. Our Vest Pocket tool makes it easy for them to search our site and then add related links of their choice -- either content they produce or posts from other contributors. The producers' desk frequently steps in to help with the work.

Bruce Upbin, a long-time FORBES editor, constantly haranged me about our inferior mobile site. I kept saying, we'll get to it. Facebook subscribe launch

February 2012

Social login/universal registration; 30M unique visitors

My greatest frustration was how long it took to enable readers to register to Forbes.com using credentials from a social site. I had hoped it would happen much earlier than this, but I badly miscalculated the under-the-hood work involved. From my T/S days, I knew social login would spur sign-ups, followers and comments. And I really wanted to scale the forum of discussion on our site. Ultimately, Mike Dugan and Thomas Solomon from our Tech team made sure this highly complicated release was the most flawless launch of my 12 years in digital publishing. The result was an immediate spike in engagement that continues to grow nicely.

June 2012

Homepage v2.0, Follow Bar, 1,000 contributors, 8,200 posts/month

June was a BIG month for the product team. The FORBES Follow Bar is a slick piece of coding work that produced an elegant user experience for one of our "big moves" -- following. It also set the stage for the release of the home page I've been awaiting for nearly two years -- and even longer since I dislike all news home pages on the Web. As much as I wanted a new front-door experience, it wasn't until I saw it in action three weeks before launch that I truly realized how it brought together four years of thinking at FORBES and T/S. It completely delivers on our promise to put our authoritative journalism at the center of a social media experience. There is nothing like it in the news category today.

July 2012

New navigation & sticky ad on phone

I'm fixated on mobile because 25% of our monthly audience is now coming from smartphones and tablets. There are a few challenges: one is getting users to engage with a smaller screen; the other is monetizing the traffic. Right now, the ad industry isn't paying much attention. But FORBES is. We continue to improve our navigation to make it easy for visitors to move from the home screen to most popular posts and new posts. We also added what we call a "sticky ad" to the bottom of each screen in a very early effort to begin to understand potential revenue models.

August 2012

New search pages & type-ahead search

We called them "blue pages." They were the last vestige of the old Forbes.com. One by one we replaced them with the more clean, open look of our article screens. When we finally got around to revamping Search pages we also introduced typeahead search, which quickly returns results for our lists about people, places and companies. There are still some blue pages out there, but they no longer are of the core Forbes.com experience.

September 2012

Universal Photo Galleries

Photos remain the crowd pleaser of the Web -- for everyone, including Facebook. Two years ago, we did a down-and-dirty overhaul of our photo gallery. It was better, but not nearly good enough. The consumer experience was clunky and it didn't meet our business needs to efficiently deliver ad impressions. Our new universal photo gallery is intended to enhance our posts, not turn text into screens to click through. Consumers will be able to scroll through galleries without leaving the page they originally chose to visit. There will soon be a hover state that makes galleries easily discoverable across our platform.
